Jeremiah Owyang · General Partner, Blitzscaling Ventures · AI-native culture keynote

Keynote · Fundraising Mechanics

Vlad Cazacu: The Goldilocks Round

Cazacu treated fundraising as financial architecture, not founder theater: the amount you raise, the milestone it must reach, and the ownership you give up all shape what happens next.

Vlad Cazacu · CEO, Flowlie Technologies · The Goldilocks Round keynote

Vlad Cazacu, CEO of Flowlie Technologies, was careful to narrow his talk to institutional venture capital. His point was that VC is a specific asset class with LP mandates, return expectations, and a defined timetable, not just a generic source of startup cash.

"Dilution is cumulative."— Vlad Cazacu, Flowlie Technologies

That logic drove the rest of the keynote. Founders should raise toward a credible next milestone, watch ownership in aggregate rather than round by round, and run fundraising like a dense sales process instead of a loose chain of meetings.

The Deep Read

Raise for the Next Station, Not the Current Label

Cazacu's keynote was a founder finance clinic. He separated institutional venture from other money, then translated that into a practical planning model: the round exists to reach the next financing milestone with enough proof, runway, and ownership left to keep going.

18-24

4-5

Relationships & Community

The Network Effect: Architecting Relationships for Career and Company Growth

Networking works best when it stops feeling like networking. The real network effect isn't transactional access — it's compounding trust built before you need it.

Innovate South 2026 · Session energy — the room that made it real

The panel pushed back on a common misunderstanding: networking is not collecting contacts. It is not walking into a room, shaking hands, and hoping something useful happens later. Done well, it is the practice of building trust before you need it.

Andy Gordon framed ecosystem-building as nonlinear. You don't enter a community, meet the "right" ten people, and finish the job. You find the front doors that already exist, show up, create collisions, and earn your way deeper through substance. Meet people broadly — then deepen through value.

Host Framing — Destin Ortego

"The value of the room is not just who is on stage. It is who you meet before, after, and between sessions. Southern hospitality can be an ecosystem advantage — if people actually use it."

"Relationships compound when you lead with value. A person who seems peripheral today may become a key customer, partner, investor, or connector years later."— Matt Wisdom, Civilized.ai

Matt Wisdom brought the operator lens: ask honest questions, learn what people are working on, understand their story, and care before there is a transaction. The best relationships aren't engineered — they grow because trust had time to develop.

Anhthu Nguyen reinforced the need for substance, especially in technical and hard-to-access spaces. Do the homework first. Lead with real interest. Resist the urge to perform expertise you don't have.

"If you can't explain the purpose of the introduction before the meeting, you may not be ready for the meeting."— Destin Ortego, Opportunity Machine

The session closed on a clear truth: networks are not built by good intentions. They are built by kept promises. If you say you are going to make an intro, send a resource, or help someone — do it.

The Deep Read

A Network Is an Operating System for Trust

This session translated relationship-building from a vague soft skill into a repeatable founder practice. The strongest advice was not to collect more contacts. It was to make fewer, better promises, protect the people who make introductions, and create enough surface area that opportunity has somewhere to land.

High Tech, High Touch: Winning Your First 100 Customers in the AI Era

AI can create more volume — more emails, more leads, more dashboards. But is it helping you find the right customer and move the right conversation forward?

Innovate South 2026 · Keynote on building agentic AI — the talk that had the room buzzing

The conversation started with a hard lesson from Casey Bolsega: more outreach doesn't always mean better traction. He described running a massive email-based outbound campaign reaching thousands of prospects monthly — only to find the channel was creating activity without meaningful results. The insight wasn't that email is dead. It was that founders must know when a channel is actually working.

Host Framing — Destin Ortego

"The more powerful the tools get, the more important the human layer becomes. AI agents and automation on one side. Roundtables, warm intros, and real founder conversations on the other. Quality control is the missing piece."

"High tech, high touch. The technology helps the team move faster. But the human still decides what matters — still coaches, calls, listens, and learns."— Adam McInturff, Mallard Bay Outdoors

Adam McInturff framed AI as a cooperative partner at Mallard Bay — using it to analyze customer signals, sales calls, website activity, and ads so the team can see patterns faster. He uses Claude as a sales coach, running call transcripts through his playbook to identify coaching moments and sharpen how objections are handled.

Amantha Bagdon of RxPost described the difficult transition from founder-led sales to training the first sales team. At RxPost, which serves pharmacies, she hired pharmacy technicians into customer-facing roles because they can relate to the pressure and pace of that world. That kind of trust is hard to automate.

Once customers start saying yes, the company needs a way to help them succeed — otherwise, every new sale creates stress instead of momentum.

Their shared argument was that AI only helps sales when it sharpens qualification, coaching, follow-up, and customer success instead of flooding the market with generic outreach.

Tech Meetups in Acadiana: Find Your Community

Acadiana's tech ecosystem isn't just made up of companies. It's made up of rooms where people ask questions, share what they know, and find the next person who helps them move forward.

Innovate South 2026 · The room that shows up every year

On April 22, 2026, this session made the invisible map visible — bringing together local organizers who are already building the rooms where Acadiana's tech community gathers and grows.

Host Framing — Destin Ortego

"People do not need to leave Louisiana to find serious tech conversations. They need better visibility into who is already building, hiring, mentoring, investing, and helping. Innovate South is how the map becomes real."

John "Zeke" Ezekiel opened with Acadiana Software Group, which meets at Opportunity Machine. It's not only for polished experts — it's a practice ground where people can present what they're learning, workshop ideas, talk about burnout, and build confidence before stepping onto larger stages.

"Opportunity doesn't strike if you never enter the room."— Hunter Thevis, S1 Technology / DEF CON Acadiana

Hunter Thevis built DEF CON Acadiana (DCG 337) from a local cybersecurity meetup into a monthly gathering with hundreds of members. People have found jobs, built business relationships, and connected across career stages through it. The goal is simple: make Acadiana a harder target by helping security professionals learn from each other.

Brandon Brown widened the map — pointing to groups across risk, auditing, critical infrastructure, women in tech, and the Lafayette Regional Tech Council, which hosts quarterly meetups for both company leaders and technical talent in separate rooms so both can speak honestly.

The meetup session connected the social side of the conference to the workforce data underneath it. Acadiana's tech ecosystem grows when people can find the rooms where practitioners gather, where students can see a path, and where employers can meet the talent they keep saying they need.

Framework

How to Find Your Community

01
Start with a real curiosity

Pick the technical, security, AI, data, or operations topic you would want to learn about even without a job posting attached.

02
Join the existing channels

Acadiana Software Group, DEF CON Acadiana, LRTC, ISACA, InfraGard, BTRSec, and other groups already create entry points.

03
Show up more than once

The first meetup gives you faces. Repeated attendance builds trust and makes follow-up less awkward.

04
Present what you are learning

Zeke's point was that presenting solidifies knowledge and gives the community another useful node.

05
Start what is missing

The panel identified room for more AI practitioner gatherings. If the room does not exist, the organizer may be in the audience.

Tech Tank 2026: Six Founders, $25,000, One Stage

No great conference ends without non-dilutive capital on the line. When preparation meets opportunity, success follows.

Tech Tank 2026 · 1st Place: Mirror Labs ($14,000) · Runner-Up: XR MediX ($8,500) · April 29, 2026

The Tech Tank competition brought six of Innovate South's best and brightest to the stage for $25,000 in non-dilutive prize money. After three days of sessions, panels, and hallway conversations, the energy in the room shifted — this was the moment founders had been building toward.

Host Framing — Destin Ortego

"This is not abstract ecosystem talk. Louisiana has specific founders solving specific problems in specific industries. The Tech Tank is where that shows up in real time — preparation meeting opportunity on a public stage."

The Deep Read

The Conference Became a Founder Stage

Tech Tank gave the recap its clearest proof point: six startups, four-minute pitches, judge Q&A, community asks, and non-dilutive money attached to the outcome. The format turned ecosystem support into something founders could actually use the next morning.

6
Founders/startups pitched on stage, representing construction, health, legal, defense, disaster response, and wellness problems.
$25K
Total non-dilutive prize pool positioned as capital founders could keep without giving up equity.
4+3
Four-minute founder pitches followed by three minutes of judge Q&A, keeping the format fast and focused.
Framework

How Tech Tank Worked

01
Pitch the company clearly

Each founder had to explain the problem, product, market, business model, traction, and team in a compressed format.

02
Make the ask to the room

The ask was aimed at community stakeholders, not just investors: introductions, customers, expertise, and connections.

03
Answer judges live

Three minutes of Q&A forced founders to defend assumptions while staying composed in public.

04
Let the room participate

The crowd favorite vote made the audience part of the process, not just passive observers.

05
Put money behind support

First place, runner-up, and crowd favorite prizes converted attention into practical startup fuel.

Winner Coverage

Mirror Labs and XR MediX

Mirror Labs

Kyle Robichaux pitched collaborative 3D job sites for construction teams, built around remote verification, LiDAR-backed spatial accuracy, issue tracking, and reducing costly rework.

Mirror's traction

The company described moving from customer discovery to a working platform in six months, with five companies testing and project integrations under discussion.

XR MediX

Prabhakar Vemavarapu pitched augmented-reality needle guidance for joint injections, aiming to improve accuracy without relying on live imaging or specialized rooms.

XR's traction

The team cited 300-plus customer discovery interviews, early adopter signups, awards, and clinical partnership needs as part of its next step.
